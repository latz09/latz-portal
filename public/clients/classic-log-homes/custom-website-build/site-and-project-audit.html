A look at Classic Log Homes

Before we talk, I wanted to do some real work first.

Megan mentioned what's going on with your site, so I spent some time on classicloghomes.biz before reaching out. Here's what I noticed, what I think the real situation is, and where I think this could go.

01 Who I am

Quick on me, then we get to your site.

I'm Jordan. I run Latz Web Design out of Stevens Point. Small operation — me and a graphic designer I trust for logos and visual design. I handle the build, the client relationship, and everything you'd actually need to call about.

That's the short version. The reason I'm spending time on this page instead of a normal email is because I think you've been around enough vendors to know the difference between someone selling you something and someone who actually looked at your business. So I looked.

02 What I noticed about Classic Log Homes

A few things stood out — some good, some leaving money on the table.

  1. 01
    The price calculator is doing most of the work.
    That tool — width, length, wall height, options — is the front door for most of your quote requests. It's the engine of the entire site. Anything we do should protect it, modernize it, and make it easier to get to from a phone.
  2. 02
    You have 50 floor plan pages that are really one template.
    Right now those pages live as 50 separate hand-built pages. On a modern site, that's one template populated by a list — meaning when you add a new plan, you fill in fields and hit save instead of duplicating a page. Same goes for filtering by bedrooms, size, or style if you ever want that.
  3. 03
    Your testimonials are buried.
    You have testimonials going back to 2007 — real names, real photos, real stories. That's the kind of social proof most companies would kill for. On your current site they're stacked in one long scroll on the homepage. They deserve their own page, organized by year, with each one given room to breathe.
  4. 04
    The Photos link in your nav goes to a dead Facebook page.
    Meanwhile, your Google Business listing already has stronger photography than your website does — that's an easy first move, just bringing those onto the site. The bigger opportunity is real photography of your actual builds. A proper shoot of two or three of your strongest packages — exterior, interior, the details people care about — would carry the entire site. That's the kind of thing buyers screenshot and send to their spouse.
  5. 05
    Three PDFs are doing the job of three real pages.
    The 2×8 log siding manual, the "Building with the Natural Tail" guide, the window and door trim measurement guide — those are real, useful documents. But Google doesn't read PDFs the way it reads webpages. Right now anyone searching for "how to measure for log siding" is finding someone else's page, not yours.
03 Where things stand

Here's how I read the situation, in plain English.

Richard's email is thorough, but it's written for another developer — not for you. The short version: your site was built on WordPress with two paid add-ons (Elementor Pro and Advanced Custom Fields Pro) running on Richard's agency licenses. Those licenses get pulled when he steps away.

You've got two real paths here. Path one is patch it — find a new host, buy your own plugin licenses, keep paying for those licenses every year, and inherit a site nobody currently maintains. Path two is treat this as the forced moment to do something better. Build a site that you actually own, on a modern foundation, with no recurring license fees, no plugin sprawl, no dependence on a stack you can't easily hand to anyone else.

On the August 1 deadline

Real, but workable. After August 1 the price calculator's admin tools and parts of the page builder stop receiving updates and you lose the ability to edit Pro elements. The site doesn't go dark — it just slowly becomes harder to change and more fragile over time.

The honest read: that gives us a 10-week window where doing this right is still very much on the table. Not panic, just a clear deadline to plan around.

04 What's possible

What a modern site does for a business like yours.

Golden Eagle is six minutes down Plover Road. They've been at it since 1966, they've got a national builder network, a design center showroom, and a website to match. You're not going to out-spend them, and you don't need to. What you need is a site that does three things well:

01 / Trust
When someone lands on your site, it loads fast, looks current, and makes them believe you're a real, modern company. Right now, the site is making them work to believe that. That's lost leads you'll never see.
02 / Conversion
The price calculator becomes easier to use on a phone (which is where most people are now). Floor plans become easier to browse. Testimonials become easier to read. Every friction point removed is a quote request that actually finishes.
03 / Reach
Those three PDFs become three real web pages. The floor plans become searchable by bedrooms, size, style. Megan can take that and run with the SEO and ads side. You start showing up for searches you're invisible for today.

You don't need a fancier brand than the other guys. You need a site that gets out of your own way. Log home buyers do real research — they're not impulse buyers — and the site that respects their time is the one they call. The math on this is straightforward: a site like this is a real investment, and for a business selling the kind of packages you sell, it pays itself back faster than most things you'll spend money on this year.

And what we build first doesn't have to be everything you want eventually. Get the foundation right — fast, modern, mobile, fully yours — and the things you might want later (3D virtual tours, a richer plan browser, deeper galleries, whatever ends up mattering as you watch how leads behave) become straightforward additions instead of another rebuild. A site you can grow into, not out of.

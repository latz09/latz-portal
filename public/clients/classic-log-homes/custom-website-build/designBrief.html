Project Snapshot

The job

Replace a decaying WordPress site with a custom build that finally speaks to both audiences Jeff serves — DIY families building their own log home, and contractors sourcing packages for clients. The old site only talks to families. The new one needs to make a contractor feel seen in the first scroll, while still pulling families in. And it has to make a two-person operation feel like the trusted Wisconsin manufacturing network it actually is.

Why this scope, not more — and what it means for both of us Phase 1 was deliberately scoped down to what proves ROI fastest: a site that actually speaks to contractors AND families, on a CMS Jeff can edit himself, ready before his old plugin licenses expire Aug 1. The plan is to land this build, prove it works, then keep adding — more floor plan content, e-commerce, calculator rebuild, business opportunities page, and a real logo project all sit waiting in Phase 2/3. If we deliver something Jeff is proud of, this becomes recurring work for both of us. So: this isn\'t the whole pie, it\'s the first slice. Design accordingly — leave room for the system to grow.

Jeff's Own Direction — Mood Board Reference

Before he hired us, Jeff was playing around with one of those AI website-builder tools trying to mock up what he wanted. I saw it before he lost access. Dark mode by default — dark background, light text, gold as the accent color. Gold borders, gold headings, gold dividers. That's where his head is.

Approximation only — the actual mockup is in the project portal. Treat these swatches as direction, not spec.

I have screenshots of sections from his mockup before access dropped. They're in the project portal under "Mood Board" — pull them before you start. Not a brief, not a spec — just where he was steering visually. Worth seeing what he was reaching for in his own words.

My read: dark + gold is the right instinct for a premium Wisconsin craftsman supplier — moves the brand away from cabin-rustic and toward grounded, confident, made-by-hand-but-not-folksy. But it's your call how literal to take it. Run with it, evolve it, or pitch a counter-direction if you see something better.

The Eagle — His "Logo"

Jeff doesn't have a real logo — what he has is this eagle image plus the words "Classic Log Homes" in a generic typeface. That's it. But the eagle matters to him. He told me he drew it himself. Not something to throw out.

A proper logo redesign isn't in this budget — and I'm not sure I'd push for one even if it were. The eagle has 22 years of meaning behind it and a personal story attached. The right move here is probably to treat the eagle with respect and build the wordmark / lockup around it — not replace it.

Your read matters here. Use it as-is? Clean up the edges? Pair it with a stronger typographic wordmark? Reserve it for the footer / favicon and let a new wordmark carry the nav? Open question — but the constraint is: the eagle stays, in some form.

Let's Talk

☕
Bring your instincts — not just answers The wireframe has done the structural thinking. Everything visual is yours. These are conversation starters for the kickoff — places where I'd rather hear your direction than hand you mine.
Open Design Questions
How do we hold the dual-audience tension visually?
A contractor and a DIY family need to feel like they're in the right place within 5 seconds. Two heroes? One hero with a smart split? Imagery that reads to both? This is the central design problem of the home page — would love to see how you'd attack it.
How literal do we take Jeff's dark + gold direction?
His own AI mockup steered dark mode + gold accent (see Mood Board in the portal). It's a good instinct — premium, confident, anti-cabin-cliché. Question is execution: how dark is dark (true black vs. warm charcoal vs. deep walnut), what kind of gold (antique brass, polished, muted ochre), and where does it earn its keep without becoming a Vegas hotel website. Would love a couple of variants on the theme rather than one locked answer.
Typography — craftsman confident, not folk-art
Needs to feel made-by-hand without looking handmade. Has to carry the headline "Wisconsin-made log home packages. Done the right way." with weight. What pairing reads as confident craftsman supplier rather than country-rustic or generic contractor?
How do we treat the eagle?
No budget for a real logo project this round, and the eagle has too much personal weight to swap out anyway (Jeff drew it). So the question is execution. Use it as-is in the nav with a stronger wordmark next to it? Clean it up subtly? Let it live in the footer / favicon while a typographic mark carries the header? Whatever direction makes the brand feel grown-up without losing what the eagle means to him.
"How We're Built" — what does authentic look like here?
This section is doing real credibility work — it's the answer to "you're just two people." Real sawmill photography is coming from Jeff, but the visual treatment around it matters. Four-icon strip, photo grid, full-bleed imagery, editorial split? How do we make a small operation feel like the network it actually is without overselling?
Floor plan card — brochure or spec sheet?
Families want emotional pull (renderings, lifestyle). Contractors want square footage, specs, downloadable PDFs. The plan card and the dynamic template both have to land for both. What's the layout that holds both jobs without splitting into two templates?

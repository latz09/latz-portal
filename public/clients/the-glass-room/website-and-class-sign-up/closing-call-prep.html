01

Opening the Call

When Sam Picks Up
Acknowledge the gap. Give permission. Ask a question.
"Hey Sam, it's Mike Latz — Latz Web Design. I know it's been a few weeks since I sent the proposal over and I didn't want to keep just emailing into the void. Totally fine if the timing's shifted or it's not the right fit anymore — I'd just rather hear that than wonder. Where are you guys at on it?"
  • Acknowledge the gap — "few weeks," not "ages." No apology, no defensiveness.
  • Permission to bail — "totally fine if it's not the right fit." Lowers her guard immediately.
  • Open question, not pitch — "where are you at on it?" forces her to answer with content, not yes/no.
  • Then shut up. Whatever she says next is the diagnosis. Don't fill silence.
If It Goes to Voicemail
Short. Low-pressure. Door open.
"Hey Sam, Mike Latz — Latz Web Design. Just checking in on The Glass Room site. No pressure either way — if the timing's shifted or you're going a different direction, totally understand. If you want to talk through it or scope it differently, give me a call back at [number]. Thanks Sam."
  • Under 25 seconds. Don't pitch the proposal again on voicemail.
  • The "going a different direction" phrasing gives her an easy out — which paradoxically makes her more likely to call back.
  • Don't reference the unanswered emails. She knows.
  • If no callback in 3–4 business days, send one short email and let it rest.
02

Reading the First 60 Seconds

Don't commit to a response track until you've heard her first real sentence. Listen for these cues to triage which scenario you're in.
→ Cue
She opens with "we've been talking…" or "we were going to call you…"
Internal conversation has happened. They've made a decision (or near-decision) and haven't told you. Almost always means budget pullback. Go to Scenario 1.
→ Cue
She immediately apologizes for not responding
The "haven't gotten to it" scenario. They're not against it, just buried. Go to Scenario 4 — gentle re-engagement with the timeline math.
→ Cue
She mentions Derek by name in the first 30 seconds
He's been the friction point. Either he's pushed back on scope/price or he's been advocating DIY. Likely Scenario 2 or 5. Don't argue Derek — agree with what's true about his perspective, then redirect.
→ Cue
She mentions "the buildout" or shop progress unprompted
Their own timeline is slipping. The site isn't the bottleneck — the physical space is. Go to Scenario 3. Reframe what a delayed launch can still do.
→ Cue
She sounds rushed, distracted, kids in background
Bad call timing. Don't pitch. Ask when's better, get a specific time, hang up. A scheduled callback at her convenience converts way better than a rushed pitch.
→ Cue
She uses past tense — "we were thinking," "we had wanted to"
Bad sign. They've mentally moved on. Go straight to Scenario 1 — explicit permission to scope down — and if that doesn't open it back up, head to the graceful exit.
→ Cue
She mentions "Squarespace," "Wix," "my cousin," or any specific alternative
Active DIY exploration. Scenario 2. Don't trash the alternative — acknowledge it honestly, then pivot to the signup system as the actual differentiator.
→ Cue
She asks a specific scope or signup-system question right away
Good sign — she's still engaged and thinking about it. They may just be deliberating. Answer the question directly, then ask what's blocking the decision.
→ Cue
Long pause before answering "where are you at on it?"
She's choosing her words to let you down gently. Beat her to it — offer the scope-down conversation before she has to deliver bad news. Scenario 1.

Response Scenarios — Re-Prioritized

Ordered by probability given the silence pattern. Lead with permission to scope down. The original proposal already laid out the levers — they know they exist.
Most Likely
#1 · Most Likely
"We've been talking and we're not sure we can do this right now"
  • Don't fight it — agree the full number is a lot mid-buildout
  • Lead with permission: "let's talk about a smaller version"
  • Walk through the $7,500 floor explicitly
  • Emphasize phased — core + signup live by mid-June, rest is Phase 2
  • The signup system is non-negotiable for opening
#2 · Likely
"We were leaning toward something cheaper / Squarespace / DIY"
  • Don't be defensive — acknowledge it can work for some pieces
  • Squarespace + café/gallery pages = fine
  • Squarespace + class signup with waiver/capacity/Stripe = doesn't exist
  • Pivot to the signup system as the actual differentiator
  • Offer to scope-down to just the signup system if that helps
Possible
#3 · Possible
"The timeline doesn't work / we're behind on buildout"
  • The site doesn't have to launch with the doors
  • Pre-launch landing page can go up in 2 weeks for email capture
  • Soft launch / "coming soon" still drives waitlist
  • A delayed site launch can be a marketing event, not a failure
  • Reframe: "what's your realistic open date now?"
#4 · Possible
"We're still thinking about it / haven't gotten to it"
  • Don't pressure — they're already feeling guilty
  • Use timeline math as the soft deadline, not a hard close
  • Offer to scope-down on the call to make the decision easier
  • Ask: "what would make this a yes or a no?"
  • Set a specific next step — date and method
#5 · Possible
Derek questions scope or pushes DIY
  • Don't argue Derek — agree with what's true
  • His marketing/design background IS valuable on this project
  • The signup system is engineering, not design — different skill
  • Offer his input on design direction during the build
  • If still pushing DIY: signup-system-only quote as fallback
If the Conversation Opens Up
Long shot
"We're ready — let's do it"
  • Don't act surprised. Move to next steps immediately.
  • Confirm scope (full or adjusted) — get specific
  • Agreement + deposit invoice goes out today
  • Kickoff within 24 hours of deposit clearing
  • Lock the timeline conversation — phased or full?
Pivot option
"What if we just did the signup system?"
  • Real option — they DIY café/gallery pages, you build the engine
  • Signup system alone ≈ $5,500–$6,500 standalone
  • Embedded into Squarespace or built on a simple landing page
  • Smaller foot in the door — relationship for Phase 2
  • Be honest: integration adds complexity vs full custom
Bonus play
She accepts the shop visit offer
  • Best possible outcome short of a yes — get on her turf
  • Lock a specific date on the call (not "next week sometime")
  • Bring laptop, wireframe, proposal, scope-adjusted versions
  • Goal: leave with a decision or a clear next step + date
  • Derek likely to be there — prep for both decision-makers

05

The Graceful Exit

If it's dead, close it cleanly. Don't push. Don't guilt. Leave the door wide open for referrals, a smaller Phase 1 later, or them coming back post-opening. A clean no today is worth more than a dragged-out maybe.

If it's clearly not happening

"Totally understand, Sam. Sounds like the timing or the budget just isn't lining up right now, and that's okay — I'd rather you do this when it's actually right than force it. Couple things before I let you go: if anything changes once you're open and you want to revisit it, even just the signup system on its own, just text me. And if you ever hear of someone in your circle who needs a site, I'd really appreciate the referral. Either way — best of luck with the opening, I'll keep an eye out for The Glass Room."
  • No guilt, no last-ditch pitch, no "are you sure?"
  • Explicitly invite a smaller future engagement (signup system alone)
  • Ask for a referral — direct, not awkward
  • Genuine wellwish for the opening — costs nothing, builds goodwill
  • End the call. Don't linger.

If it's a "maybe later" — leave a specific door open

"Sounds like now isn't the moment, but it's not a no either. How about this — let's circle back in [specific timeframe, e.g. 'mid-June' or 'after you're open']. I'll send a calendar hold so it doesn't just disappear, and you can decide then with more information. Sound fair?"
  • Specific date beats "let's keep in touch"
  • You send the calendar hold — don't make her do the work
  • Frame the follow-up as "you decide then with more info" — low pressure
  • If she agrees, set it before the call ends

Post-call note to yourself

Log the call outcome immediately. Even if it's dead, capture: what scenario did it turn out to be? Did the diagnostics work? What did Sam actually say in the first 60 seconds? That data sharpens the next cold call.

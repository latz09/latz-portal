Brand & Creative Direction

🎨
Replacing a Fiverr logo — this is a clean slate Current logo is raster-only, wrong file formats, doesn't carry authority. Bill described it as "plain and mostly files are wrong." Nothing to carry forward from the existing mark. Full creative freedom on direction.
Color Direction — Starting Points
Base
Navy — the anchor. Federal, serious, authoritative.
Accent
Gold/brass — authority, credibility. Not flashy — earned.
Patriotic
Red — subtle patriotic undertone. Not a flag — a nod.
White — clean, open
Client Says"Stay with simple colors." — Bill wants clean and strong, not complex or trendy. These are starting points — refine the exact palette based on what reads best at every size.
Brand Personality
In Their Words"Professional and trustworthy and can get the job done."
ToneAuthoritative but not cold. Competent, reliable, no-nonsense. The kind of company you trust with a $2M federal subcontract.
What It's NotNot flashy, not trendy, not startup-y. Nothing that looks cheap, AI-generated, busy, or hard to read. Bill specifically called those out.
First Impression"Professional and trustworthy" — that's the gut check. If a procurement officer doesn't feel that in the first second, we missed.
Logo Direction
TypeMonogram (PFL) with full company name underneath. They like the idea of both working together.
EmbroideryMust be embroidery-friendly — no fine detail, no thin lines, no gradients. This constrains line weight and complexity.
Flexibility"We are open to anything." Full creative latitude. Present 2–3 distinct directions including the monogram approach.
TypographyStrong modern sans-serif implied by the overview. No playful or casual fonts. Needs to read "federal contractor," not "local shop."
Tagline
Candidate"Compliance and Coordination You Can Count On"
StatusWorking idea from the client. Build a tagline lock-up as part of the logo system — needs to work with and without.
NotesIt's functional and descriptive, which fits the federal audience. Could be refined — but start designing with it and we'll adjust if needed.
Anti-References — What to Avoid
Busy"Ones with a lot going on and hard to read" — keep it clean and legible at every size
Cheap"Look cheap and simple AI made" — nothing that reads as template or auto-generated
No InspirationBill didn't have specific logos he admires. No competitor brands to reference either — do your own competitive scan to make sure PFL stands apart in the federal logistics space.

Let's Talk

☕
Bring your instincts — not just answers These are conversation starters for when we kick off concepts. You know design better than I do — if you've got a direction you want to run with after reading this, let's hear it.
Creative Questions
How do we make "PFL" feel authoritative as a monogram without looking generic?
They want a PFL monogram with the full name underneath. The risk is it looks like every other three-letter logistics company. What makes this monogram feel like it belongs on a DoD subcontractor's capability statement? Weight, structure, negative space — what's the play?
Embroidery-friendly from day one — how does that shape the design?
Bill specifically called out embroidered shirts. That means no fine lines, no gradients, limited detail at small sizes. Better to design with that constraint baked in than to simplify a complex logo after the fact. What line weight and complexity level gives us the best of both worlds?
Navy + gold + patriotic undertones — how do we execute this without looking like a military branch or a law firm?
The palette direction is clear. The risk is ending up in "generic government contractor" territory. How do we use these colors in a way that feels modern and distinct? Where does the red play — if at all? What separates this from the dozens of navy-and-gold shield logos in the federal space?
What typography says "federal contractor" without saying "boring"?
Strong modern sans-serif is the direction. But there's a range between Helvetica-safe and something with more character. What font pairings feel authoritative enough for a capability statement but distinctive enough to be a real brand?
The tagline: "Compliance and Coordination You Can Count On" — does it work as a lock-up?
It's long. It's descriptive. It might work well on a capability statement or website but be too much for a business card lock-up. Worth thinking about whether we design a full lock-up version or suggest they shorten it. Or both.
No competitor references, no admired brands — what does the landscape actually look like?
Bill said "none really" to both questions. That means we need to do our own homework. What are federal logistics and compliance firms actually doing with their branding? Where's the visual gap PFL can own? A quick scan before concepts start would prevent us from accidentally looking like someone else.
What should the icon mark communicate at 32px without the company name?
The PFL monogram is one approach, but the favicon and social profile picture need something even simpler. What reads at that scale? A single letter? An abstract mark? The monogram shrunk down? Worth having a plan before we're in revisions trying to make a complex mark work small.

Project Snapshot

The job

Give Premier Federal Logistics a web presence that matches its credentials. Tracy has secret-level DoD clearance and transported M1 Abrams tanks. Bill has 1M+ accident-free miles. They subcontract under Boeing, Halliburton, and UPS — and right now a procurement officer searching for PFL finds nothing. This site needs to fix that in 10 seconds flat: credibility, authority, and the kind of visual weight that makes someone confident enough to pick up the phone.

Let's Talk

☕
Bring your instincts — not just answers These are things worth talking through before we're deep in build mode. You've also got the brand brief — the logo and site design should feel like one coherent system. If you've got a direction you want to run with after reading both briefs, let's hear it.
Open Design Questions
How does the brand identity translate into a full website design system?
You're building the logo, color palette, and typography in the brand project — then those decisions become the foundation for 9 pages. What's your approach for making that handoff seamless? Think about how the navy/gold/red palette scales across hero sections, cards, CTAs, and dense content pages like Services.
What does the About/Leadership page look like when it's the most important page on the site?
Tracy's credentials include secret-level DoD clearance and M1 Abrams transport. Bill has 1M+ accident-free miles and 40+ property rehabs. These bios are genuinely elite — they're not LinkedIn fluff. How do we design around them so a procurement officer reading this page says "these people are the real deal"? Headshot placement, credential callouts, visual hierarchy — this page needs to do maximum work.
How do we handle the hero without professional photos yet?
Photography is being coordinated separately and may not be ready when design starts. Stock imagery is the fallback for now. What's the hero strategy — dark overlay on stock, abstract pattern, solid-color authority treatment? Worth having a plan that works with or without real photography, since we'll swap in real photos when they arrive.
Trust badges and certification placements — how prominent is too prominent?
Veteran-owned, EDWOSB, WOSB, CAGE code, Secret-level DoD, ARCOM — there's a lot of trust signal content. Procurement officers expect to see these. But stacking badges and credentials too aggressively can feel desperate. What's the right visual approach for authority without looking like you're overcompensating?
Service landing pages — what makes a deep-dive page convert?
Three standalone service pages built for SEO and outreach. These need to work as landing pages someone might hit directly from a search or a shared link — not just subpages of the main site. What's the structure that makes a procurement officer who lands on /services/mission-tracking take the next step?
The Contact page has a multi-field form with dropdowns — how do we keep it from feeling like a government form?
Organization type dropdown, services of interest dropdown, name, company, email, phone, message. That's a lot of fields. The audience fills out government forms all day — this needs to feel like a conversation, not paperwork. How do we design the form experience so it's inviting rather than bureaucratic?

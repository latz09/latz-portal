URLstonebridgemarketing.com

~6 weeks from first contact to full brain dump. Organic lead through Founders Network. She tracked Jordan down from a business card — that's a strong signal. Meeting was relaxed, genuine, no pressure. She's smart, self-sufficient, has opinions, builds things with AI tools, and knows what she wants aesthetically. She just needs someone who can do it properly. This is a great client if you can make Phase 1 affordable enough to start.

The AI Mockup Situation

She built a full mockup site with Base44, inspired by Stonebridge Marketing. It's actually good — Cormorant Garamond + Jost, rose/sage/teal palette, editorial feel, solid layout structure. Her concern: "I don't know where the info is stored." That's the pitch — custom-built, Vercel hosting she controls, her domain, her code, no mystery platforms. Don't make her feel dumb for using AI. Reference the mockup positively and show her what a real developer adds: performance, SEO, working integrations, code ownership.

Budget Reality

She was direct about it: can't drop $4k at once, husband needs a truck. The $3–3.5k ballpark didn't make her flinch but she also didn't commit on the spot. The proposal needs to make Phase 1 feel like an investment that replaces what she's already spending (GoDaddy + a site that doesn't work for her), not a new expense. If she's paying monthly for GoDaddy + multiple platforms that don't talk to each other, Phase 1 alone might be cheaper in the long run.

Her Communication Style

She's warm, honest, self-deprecating ("better late than never, right? 😅"), and writes in a way that feels like talking. She's not going to ghost — she just took 3 weeks to send the email because she's busy running a business. She apologized for the delay. She's engaged and invested. Communication through email works fine. Don't overstructure things — match her energy.

The "Don't Feel Sold To" Principle

This is the single most important design principle for the site. She said it twice: "I don't want people to feel sold to — I want them to feel like they already decided and they are here to figure out the next steps." That means: no flashing Book Now buttons in the nav, no urgency tactics, no countdown timers, no "limited availability" badges (unless genuinely seasonal). The CTAs live on the pages themselves, in context, after the content has done its job. The nav should feel like a table of contents, not a conversion machine.

HoneyBook — Don't Replace It

She uses HoneyBook for invoicing, proposals, contracts, and payments. She didn't say she hates it. She just wants it connected to a site that actually represents her. ~29% of HoneyBook users are photographers. It has embeddable forms, integrates with Calendly and Pic-Time, and recently added direct Pic-Time integration. Don't scope replacing it. Embed it. Let the tools she already knows do what they do. Phase 1 is a front door, not a platform swap.

The $10k+ Number

The full vision (website + portal + payment + consolidation) is real but it lives in the future. Show it in the proposal as a roadmap, not a price tag. The worst outcome is she sees $10k and goes back to Base44. Frame it as: here's Phase 1 (you can say yes today), here's where we're going when you're ready. Give her a roadmap, not a quote to choke on.

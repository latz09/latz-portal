01 — First Impression

What a procurement contact sees in the first 5 seconds.

A facilities manager evaluating you for a $200K+ project is making a snap judgment about whether you're a serious operator. Here's what your homepage is telling them right now.

!
Site is built on GoDaddy Website Builder, copyright still reads 2018
Fix This
The HTML meta tag identifies the platform as "Starfield Technologies; Go Daddy Website Builder 8.0.0000" — the lowest-end DIY builder. Footer reads © 2018. To a procurement officer evaluating contractors for a multi-state facility roll-out, this signals the company hasn't updated anything in years.
!
Homepage headline glitches and renders 3 times in a row
Fix This
"Trusted Pros in Commercial Painting" appears three times stacked on top of each other on the homepage. This is a visual bug in the GoDaddy template — and it's the first thing a visitor sees above the fold.
!
Homepage leads with a job application, not a quote request
Fix This
A facilities manager hitting the homepage is greeted with a "Join SDS Painting — Start Your Career Today" employment form. The path to actually hiring SDS for a project is buried. The two visitor types you serve (buyers and recruits) need entirely different first impressions.
~
Only 4 pages — Home, About, Gallery, Contact
Worth Noting
No dedicated pages for your core services (tank coating, industrial sandblasting, vinyl wall coverings, epoxy flooring). No industry verticals (food processing, manufacturing, healthcare). No project case studies. For a contractor with your client list, this is leaving a massive amount of credibility on the table.
✓
Brand mark itself is solid
Working Well
The "SDS Painting Co." red wordmark is clean, professional, and works well at small sizes. The brand identity is one of the few things on the current site that doesn't need to be rebuilt from scratch.

03 — Trust Signals

Proof that you can actually do the work.

Your client list is the strongest asset in the building. The site does almost nothing with it.

~
Great project photos exist — locked inside an outdated carousel, used nowhere else
Worth Noting
The Gallery page has 50+ legitimately strong project photos — real commercial and industrial work that shows the scale of what SDS does. The problem isn't the photos. The problem is the photos live in a clunky GoDaddy carousel on one page and aren't used anywhere else on the site. The homepage has none. The About page has stock-feeling shots. The client list has no images attached. The single most powerful asset SDS already owns is hidden two clicks deep.
!
Two visible typos in the client list — including hospital and college names
Fix This
The About page client list reads "Aspirus Hostipal" and "Mid-State Techincal College." These are real organizations you've worked for — and you're misspelling their names on a site that hasn't been edited in years. A procurement officer who notices this assumes the same attention to detail applies to your job sites.
!
Zero testimonials, zero case studies, zero project descriptions
Fix This
You list 16 major accounts including the Packers, Sentry, Sherwin Williams, Marshfield Clinic, and Stevens Point Brewery. None have a story attached. No before/after, no scope, no quote from a facilities manager. The client list is reduced to a wall of text bullets when it should be the single most powerful sales tool on the site.
!
No safety credentials, insurance info, or compliance documentation visible
Fix This
For commercial and industrial work, buyers are looking for OSHA, EMR scores, bonding capacity, insurance certificates, and union/non-union status before they'll put you on a bid list. Your site shows none of it. Competitors are leading with this exact content.
~
Google reviews: 4.0 stars across 6 reviews
Worth Noting
The rating is solid. The volume is the problem. For a 30-year business, six reviews suggests no system in place for asking. The reviews you have are buried — they don't appear anywhere on the website itself.
~
Facebook link repeats 6 times in the footer
Worth Noting
Another GoDaddy template artifact — the same Facebook URL appears six times in the footer in a vertical stack. Minor on its own, but it's the kind of detail that adds up to "this company doesn't pay attention."
Something I can fix alongside the site
Six reviews in thirty years isn't a quality problem — it's a process problem.
After every completed job, your project lead opens a tool on their phone, enters the facilities contact's name, and sends them a personalized review request with a direct link to your Google page. Tap, done. I can build that tool for you alongside the new site — a simple internal tool tied to your Google Business Profile, designed for the way your crews actually work. Your review count starts climbing within weeks.
04 — Lead Capture

How a real lead is supposed to reach you.

Someone managing a $500K facility repaint shouldn't have to dig to figure out how to get a bid from you. Right now, they do.

!
Contact form asks for two fields: name and email
Fix This
The Contact Us form is labeled "Get Your Commercial Paint" but only collects Name and Email. No phone, no project type, no facility size, no timeline, no message field, no location, no budget range. You're capturing 10% of the information a bid request actually needs, which means every inquiry requires a follow-up email just to figure out what they want.
!
Business email is sdspaint@gmail.com — not a domain email
Fix This
For an enterprise-targeted painting contractor, sending bids and proposals from a @gmail.com address (instead of @sdspaintingcompany.com) is the kind of detail Fortune 500 procurement teams notice. It's a small fix with outsized credibility impact.
~
Primary CTA is a generic "Get in Touch" button that links to the same page
Worth Noting
The "Get in Touch" button on the homepage actually links back to the homepage itself — it doesn't go anywhere. A clear "Request a Bid" or "Get a Project Estimate" CTA pointing to a real intake form would convert dramatically better.
✓
Phone number is click-to-call and visible on every page
Working Well
715-342-9792 is tappable on mobile, which is exactly right — though it deserves a more prominent placement and a clearer CTA treatment than the generic text-link styling it has now.
05 — Search & Visibility

You own Stevens Point. You're invisible everywhere else.

The most interesting finding in this audit. Your local search position is actually strong — but the moment a buyer searches for the bigger industrial market, you disappear.

✓
#1 in Google Maps for "commercial painter Stevens Point WI"
Working Well
For your immediate home market, you're the top result in the local pack. Thirty years of local presence is doing the heavy lifting here. The good news: you don't have to fight for this. The catch: this isn't where the $500K+ industrial work comes from.
✓
#3 in organic Google results for the same query
Working Well
Decent organic placement on the local term. Again — defending the home market is going fine.
!
Not ranking organically for "industrial painter Wisconsin"
Fix This
The local pack shows you at #1, but the organic results — the ones that capture out-of-town facility managers, multi-site procurement teams, and corporate buyers — don't include SDS at all. The exact buyers most likely to hire you for $200K+ projects can't find you when they look beyond Stevens Point.
!
No location-based or service-based pages exist on the site
Fix This
You serve Wausau, Appleton, Green Bay, the Fox Valley, and Northern Wisconsin — but Google has no way to know that. No service-area pages. No service-specific pages (industrial sandblasting, tank coating, epoxy flooring). When someone in Appleton searches for "tank coating Wisconsin," you don't show up because the website never tells Google you do that work.
!
Business address inconsistent across the web
Fix This
Your own site lists 2220 Minnesota Avenue. Yelp lists 1717 Strongs Ave. Other directories show 3440B Church St. This NAP (Name/Address/Phone) inconsistency confuses Google's local algorithm and quietly hurts your map ranking — especially as you try to expand into adjacent markets.
~
Outdated company descriptions floating across the web
Worth Noting
Third-party listings describe the company as "two 5,000 sq ft shops" and "over 200,000 yards of vinyl wall coverings" — both figures the company has long since outgrown (current numbers are 10,000 sq ft and 300,000+ yards). Old marketing copy is still doing your introductions for you.

07 — My Take

The honest read.

Jordan's Assessment
SDS has a thirty-year reputation problem online: nobody knows about it.

Most websites I audit have business problems on top of website problems — they're losing in their market because their offering is weak or their reviews are bad. That's not the situation here. The business itself is strong: 30 years, the Packers, Marshfield Clinic, $200K–$1M+ projects, a 10,000 sq ft facility, a skilled team. The new ownership inherits real assets.

The problem is purely about presentation. The current site was built on a free DIY tool in 2018, never updated, and is now actively misrepresenting what this company is capable of. A facilities manager at a Fortune 500 manufacturer looking for a contractor for a multi-year painting program lands on this site and bounces in 10 seconds — not because SDS can't do the work, but because the site doesn't look like a company that does it.

The credibility gap between what SDS actually is and how it appears online is the largest I've encountered in any audit. That gap is what every modern-looking competitor in your service radius gets to take advantage of by default.

The good news: the home market is defensible (you're #1 in maps locally), the brand is recognized in the region, and the client list is genuinely elite. A modern site doesn't need to invent credibility — it needs to finally show the credibility that's already there.

The window: new ownership is the right moment to make this investment. Procurement contacts who haven't reconsidered SDS in five years will see the rebrand. The regional bids you're currently losing by default — to contractors with worse credentials and better websites — become competitive again.

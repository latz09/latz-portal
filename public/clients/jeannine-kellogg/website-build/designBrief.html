Project Snapshot

The job

Rebuild jeanninekellogg.com from a static author page into a trilogy-first ecommerce site. Jeannine writes middle-grade fantasy — The Tukor Series. Book 1 is published and ready to sell. Books 2 and 3 are in production. A parent meets Jeannine at a book fair, searches her name later, and needs to land on a real author site with a buy button. Right now there's no way to purchase online. That changes with this build.

Client & Audience

The Client
Who She IsSelf-published author from the Twin Cities. Publishes under Jagged Compass Publishing (Jagged Compass LLC). Middle-grade fantasy trilogy — The Tukor Series.
Her StorySurvived a publishing catastrophe in late 2025 — lost a 2,400-book customer, catastrophic defective print run. Came back, expanded to a trilogy, hired a marketing agency. She fights for her work.
Decision MakerJeannine — solo. No board, no committee. She decides and moves.
ToneImmersive, story-driven, cinematic. This is a fantasy world — the site should feel like stepping into it.
The Audience
PrimaryParents of middle-school kids. They meet Jeannine at book fairs / school visits, then search her name later to buy.
SecondaryMiddle-school readers (10–14) who discover the books on their own. The site needs to feel exciting to a kid, not just functional for a parent.
Key BehaviorIn-person discovery → online purchase. The site bridges that gap. If there's no buy button, the sale dies.
DevicesParents on mobile at a book fair, kids on tablets/laptops at home. Mobile-first matters here.
💡
Vibe in one sentence Stepping into a fantasy world — epic, immersive, cinematic. Think illustrated storybook meets polished author platform. Not a generic book-listing site, not a Squarespace template. An experience that makes a 12-year-old want to read and a parent feel confident clicking "buy."

Let's Talk

☕
This one's different — brand is in motion Fuzzy Duck is building the brand identity in parallel. We can't wait for them to finish, so design decisions need to be flexible. Think of it as designing a beautiful frame that's ready to receive a painting. Your instincts on color, type, and tone set the direction — Fuzzy Duck's assets slot in when they arrive. These questions are conversation starters, not a checklist.
Open Design Questions
How do we keep "immersive fantasy world" while adding a shopping cart?
The v1 site feels like stepping into the story — full-width artwork panels, dramatic illustrations, cinematic flow. Now we're adding "Add to Cart" buttons, a cart drawer, and a Shop page. How does ecommerce live inside a fantasy world without breaking the spell? The cart needs to feel like part of the experience, not a jarring switch to storefront mode.
What's the color direction before Fuzzy Duck delivers?
No palette exists yet. The current v1 site uses dark tones that let the artwork pop. Fuzzy Duck will deliver brand colors mid-May — but we're designing now. What direction feels right for a fantasy trilogy about faraway lands, volcanoes, tundra, and dangerous creatures? Something that works as a foundation and can absorb a brand palette later without a full redesign.
Trilogy showcase — three books reading as one story
Jeannine's confirmed direction: the three books are "one big long story" — not three standalone novels. The homepage needs to communicate this immediately. How do we visually connect the books as chapters of one epic narrative? Think progression, continuity, a visual throughline — not three equal product cards sitting next to each other.
Typography that serves both kids and parents
The primary readers are middle-schoolers (10–14). The primary buyers are their parents. The site needs to feel exciting and adventurous to a kid while looking credible and trustworthy to a parent. What typeface pairing threads that needle? Something with character that doesn't tip into childish or overly polished.
How does the "Coming Soon" state for Book 3 feel special, not empty?
The book page template has a "Coming Soon" toggle — Book 3 launches in that state. It shows the stormy-sea cover and a brief "final battle approaches" message, but none of the full-page components (synopsis, artwork panels, gallery, purchase). How do we design this toggled-down state so it feels intentional and exciting rather than like a broken version of the real page? This needs to build anticipation for the finale.
What happens in the nav before a logo exists?
Fuzzy Duck is designing the logo. It's not here yet and we can't wait. The nav needs an identity element — "The Tukor Series" wordmark? A Madsen illustration detail? Something that feels intentional now and can be swapped for the real logo later without breaking the layout.
